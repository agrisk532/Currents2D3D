Chemical model for superconductivity has electron pair currents in the ground state

 

Sven Larsson and Agris Klimkans

Department of Physical Chemistry

Chalmers University of Technology

S-41296 Goteborg, Sweden

 

A chemical model for superconductivity has been derived in earlier papers. Here we show that this model explains a number of experimental data, such as electron pair currents in the ground state.

 

1. INTRODUCTION

Although the BCS model accounts for some important physical mechanisms, such as the coupling between electronic and nuclear motions, it is not possible to predict from it which systems may be superconducting. We have developed a 'chemical model' which is based on accurate quantum chemical calculations for a 4-site problem. The model is extended to very large clusters and gives results consistent with d-type superconductivity. The point of departure is that activationless transfer of electron pairs must be possible. From this follows that three oxidation states must be accessible for the essential metal ion. More generally there must be a subsystem A, for example an organic molecule in organic superconductors or a metal ion, capable of disproportionation of the type:

2A ® A+ + A-

The structural differences between A, A+, and A- should be small. Such a mechanism appears relevant for many high-Tc superconductors. The appearance of Meissner effect [2] requires that the electronic ground state permits currents as outlined by London [3]. We have demonstrated that a square planar, one electron per site system, may permit currents of d-type where the carriers are electron pairs (figs. 1 and 2). We believe that there are other structures which permit s-type or p-type superconductivity. The occurence of currents is consistent with the condition of three oxidation states, since activationless electron pair transfer is possible only if the spin density wave (SPD) (antiferromagnetic) and charge density wave (CDW) states are close in energy. 'Resonance' between the two states provide the 'condensation energy'.

 

2. RESULTS

The program is of tight-binding type in a perpendicular magnetic field using London gauge [4], and simulates ab initio methods which can only be applied for 4-site clusters. The currents are proportional to the imaginary part of the bond order and quite independent of molecular orbital method used [5]. There is no reason to believe that the result would be qualitatively different had it been possible to use a very accurate quantum chemical method for large clusters.

It should be pointed out that the currents obtained are not ordinary diamagnetic currents of Landau type [6], but involve all the valence electrons of a Van Hove singularity. The currents lead to a stronger field in the central part of the square (figs.1,2). In the infinite plane the effect should be diamagnetic in the same way as Landau diamagnetism [6,7].

The currents calculated here from first principles are reminescent of the phenomenological Ginzburg-Landau currents [8]. Closer comparison will be done in forthcoming papers. A vortex may be explained by the lower orbital energy obtained for a stronger field. The orbitals in next layer circulating around a vortex will be occupied, strengthening the vortex.
